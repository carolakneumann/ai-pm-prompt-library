# 03 — Executive Stakeholder Alignment

## What this is for
Get executive sponsor buy-in on roadmap decisions before 
the room turns against you.

## When to use it
- Before any executive presentation or roadmap review
- When you sense resistance from a key stakeholder
- When competing priorities are creating tension at the top
- When you need to pre-align before a big decision meeting

## The Prompt

<role>
You are a senior product executive coach who has helped PMs 
navigate stakeholder politics at large financial institutions. 
You know that executive alignment isn't about having the best 
deck — it's about understanding what each sponsor needs to 
feel confident saying yes. You help PMs get ahead of objections 
before they become blockers.
</role>

<context>
I am a Senior PM at Wells Fargo preparing to align executive 
sponsors on the following roadmap decision:

The decision: [describe what you need approval or alignment on]

Executive sponsor 1: [NAME/ROLE]
- What they care about most: [e.g. risk, revenue, compliance]
- Their likely objection: [what you think they'll push back on]
- Their relationship to this initiative: [supportive / neutral / resistant]

Executive sponsor 2: [NAME/ROLE]
- What they care about most: [e.g. risk, revenue, compliance]
- Their likely objection: [what you think they'll push back on]
- Their relationship to this initiative: [supportive / neutral / resistant]

What I need from them: [e.g. approval, resources, air cover]
What happens if I don't get alignment: [the risk or consequence]
</context>

<task>
Help me get executive alignment before the formal review. 
Think step by step:

STEP 1 — STAKEHOLDER MOTIVATION MAP
For each executive sponsor, identify:
- What does success look like for them personally?
- How does this initiative help or threaten that success?
- What is the minimum they need to feel comfortable saying yes?

STEP 2 — OBJECTION ANTICIPATION
List the top 3 objections I am likely to face in the room.
For each write a one-paragraph response that is direct, 
evidence-backed, and respectful of their concerns.

STEP 3 — PRE-ALIGNMENT STRATEGY
Write a pre-meeting strategy for each sponsor.
What should I say in a 1:1 before the formal review?
What should I ask vs tell?
What should I never say in the room?

STEP 4 — THE OPENING STATEMENT
Write a 2-minute opening statement for my executive review 
that leads with what sponsors care about most, states the 
decision clearly, and names the trade-offs honestly.

STEP 5 — IF IT GOES WRONG
Write 3 de-escalation responses I can use if the room 
turns against the proposal. Each should acknowledge 
the concern, hold the position, and redirect to evidence.
</task>

<output_format>
Use headers for each step.
Keep language direct and politically aware — this is a 
high stakes conversation not a product review.
The opening statement in Step 4 should be something I can 
deliver confidently without notes.
</output_format>

## Notes
- The more honest you are about objections the better the output
- Step 3 is the most valuable — pre-alignment wins more rooms 
than perfect decks
- Step 5 is your insurance policy — prepare it even if you 
don't think you'll need it
- Never go into an executive review without having done 
Step 3 first
